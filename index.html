Paragliding Q&A

You don’t need to be an athlete, but a basic level of fitness helps. You should be able to run a few steps for takeoff and landing. Be prepare to carry your paragliding equipment, which can be relatively heavy (around 15-20kg). If you have any health concerns, consult a doctor before flying.

Light to moderate winds (5-15 mph) with a stable direction are ideal for easy takeoff and landing. Sunny days with good thermal activity—rising columns of warm air that provide lift—allow for sustained flight. Avoid flying in strong winds, storms, or turbulent conditions. Early mornings and late afternoons often offer the best weather for paragliding.

A basic beginner’s course usually takes 5-7 days. To fly solo safely, you’ll need further practice and certification, which can take several weeks or months depending on your progress.

In most countries, you need a certification from a recognized training school to paraglide legally. Some places require a formal license, while others allow paragliding in designated areas without one. For example, in the UK, a license isn’t required, but you must follow specific regulations, such as obtaining the landowner’s permission before flying. Always check the local aviation laws and regulations before you fly.

If a paraglider fails, the pilot will typically experience a sudden loss of lift, potentially causing a rapid descent. Depending on the severity of the failure, this could result in a hard landing or, in extreme cases, a crash. However, most experienced pilots carry a reserve parachute, which can be deployed in emergencies to reduce the impact. Proper training and regular practice are essential for pilots to effectively handle emergencies and minimise the risk of injury in the event of a paraglider failure.

Experienced paragliders can cover impressive distances, depending on weather conditions, terrain, and flying techniques. On average, cross-country flights range between 50 and 200 kilometres. The duration of a flight also varies, with recreational flights typically lasting between 15 minutes and an hour, while cross-country flights can extend to three to six hours. Factors such as thermal activity, wind direction, and terrain features like mountain ridges and valleys play a crucial role in determining how far and how long a paraglider can stay in the air.
